For an independent car dealer, a vehicle's provenance is just as critical as its mechanical condition. Buying a car with a hidden past can result in severe financial loss, legal liability, and irreversible damage to your dealership's reputation.

The standard defense against this is the HPI Check. While the term "HPI Check" is a registered trademark of cap hpi, it has become the generic industry term for a comprehensive vehicle history check.

Here is a definitive guide to what an HPI check actually covers, which markers you must never ignore, and how modern software is automating this critical process.

## What Does an HPI Check Actually Reveal?

A full provenance check queries multiple databases, including the DVLA, the Police National Computer (PNC), the Motor Insurance Anti-Fraud and Theft Register (MIAFTR), and major finance houses. 

A comprehensive check will flag:

### 1. Outstanding Finance
This is the most common and arguably the most dangerous marker. If a vehicle has outstanding finance (usually HP or PCP), the finance company still legally owns the car. If you buy it and the previous owner stops paying, the finance company can repossess the car from your forecourt, leaving you entirely out of pocket.
*Note:* A "Unit Stocking" marker simply means another dealer currently has the car funded on their forecourt plan. This is standard trade practice and clears upon sale.

### 2. Written-Off Status (Insurance Categories)
If a car has been declared a total loss by an insurance company, it will carry a category marker.
- **Cat A & B:** These cars must be crushed or broken for parts. They can never return to the road.
- **Cat S (Structurally damaged) & Cat N (Non-structurally damaged):** These can be repaired and returned to the road. While you *can* legally sell them, their retail value is significantly lower (often 20-30% below book price), and you are legally obligated to disclose this status to the buyer.

### 3. Stolen Status
The check queries the Police National Computer. If a car is flagged as stolen, you cannot legally buy or sell it. You must report it immediately.

### 4. Mileage Discrepancies
The system checks the National Mileage Register (NMR) and historical MOT records. If a car had 60,000 miles in 2023 but currently displays 45,000 miles, the check will flag a discrepancy, indicating "clocking."

### 5. Number Plate Changes & Export/Import
It tracks whether the vehicle has had private plates assigned to it, or if it was originally registered outside the UK (an import), which can heavily affect insurance premiums and retail value.

## The "Condition Alert" vs. a True Marker

Dealers often get spooked by a "Condition Alert" on a check. This simply means the vehicle was subject to a total loss claim *before* the current category system (Cat S/N) was introduced in 2017, or it was deemed a total loss by an insurer but not officially recorded on MIAFTR. 

Treat a condition alert with the same caution as a Cat N/S marker. Inspect the car thoroughly and adjust your valuation accordingly.

## Why Manual Checks Are Failing Dealers

In the past, dealers would log into a separate portal, type in the VRM, pay £5, print the PDF, and stick it in a physical deal file.

This manual process is flawed:
1. **Human Error:** If a busy sales exec forgets to run the check during a part-exchange appraisal, you risk buying a financed car.
2. **Data Silos:** The check sits in a filing cabinet, meaning it isn't automatically referenced when the vehicle is published to your website.
3. **Outdated Information:** A car might be clear when you buy it, but if a finance house is slow to update the register, a marker could appear two days later.
