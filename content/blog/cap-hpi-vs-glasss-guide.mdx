For decades, the UK motor trade has relied on two massive data sets to determine the wholesale and retail value of a vehicle: **cap hpi** (often just referred to as CAP) and **Glass's Guide**.

If you ask ten independent dealers which platform is more accurate, you'll get ten different answers, often followed by a heated debate about specific vehicle models where the two guides wildly disagree.

In 2026, the landscape of vehicle valuations has shifted. Here is an objective comparison of cap hpi and Glass's Guide, and how independent dealers should be using them in the modern market.

## 1. The Heritage and Methodology

Both companies have deep roots in the motor trade, but they arrived at their data differently.

**Glass's Guide:** 
Launched in 1933, Glass's was the original "dealer's bible." Historically, it relied heavily on editorial expertise—a team of industry veterans adjusting values based on their knowledge of the market. Today, under the ownership of Autovista Group, Glass's relies on a massive influx of daily data, but it still retains a slightly more traditional "retail-down" approach in its algorithms.

**cap hpi:**
Cap (Car Auction Point) was founded in 1979 specifically to track auction data. It has traditionally been a "wholesale-up" model. Because it is so heavily integrated into the UK's major auction houses (like BCA), its "CAP Clean" figure has become the de facto currency of the trade. If you are standing at an auction, the auctioneer is referencing CAP.

## 2. Accuracy: Which is "Right"?

There is no definitive "right" book. The accuracy of either platform depends entirely on the specific vehicle segment and the current volatility of the market.

**Where CAP Excels:**
- High-volume fleet vehicles (Ford Focus, VW Golf, Nissan Qashqai). Because CAP sees thousands of these passing through the auction blocks every week, its data is incredibly sharp and reactive.
- Short-term wholesale volatility. If the bottom falls out of the diesel market on a Tuesday, CAP's live data will reflect it almost immediately.

**Where Glass's Excels:**
- Niche, prestige, and older vehicles. Where auction data is sparse, Glass's editorial algorithms often provide a more realistic retail expectation.
- Commercial vehicles and vans, where Glass's traditionally held a very strong position.

## 3. The Problem with "Book Price" in 2026

The biggest issue with both CAP and Glass's is that they are, fundamentally, national averages. 

If you are valuing a BMW 3 Series, the book price tells you what the "average" UK dealer paid for it yesterday. But it doesn't tell you what a buyer in *your specific town* is willing to pay for it *tomorrow*.

In 2026, relying purely on a national book price is dangerous. The internet has localized demand. 
- A ULEZ-compliant petrol hatchback is worth significantly more in London than the book price suggests.
- A 4x4 pickup is worth more in the Scottish Highlands than the book price suggests.

## 4. How Dealerships Should Use Valuations Today

To maximize margin and minimize risk, independent dealers need to blend wholesale book data with live retail market data.

**The Strategy:**
1. **Use CAP as your floor:** When appraising a part-exchange or bidding at auction, use "CAP Clean" or "CAP Average" as your absolute baseline. This is the currency of the trade. If you pay CAP Clean, you know you can likely trade it out at BCA for close to that figure if you make a mistake.
2. **Use Live Retail Data for your ceiling:** Do not use CAP Retail or Glass's Retail to set your asking price. Use software (like ForecourIQ's Command Centre) that scans AutoTrader and local dealer websites to see what identical cars are actually listed for within a 30-mile radius.
3. **Calculate the spread:** If CAP Clean is £10,000, and live local retail data shows the car selling for £11,500, you have a £1,500 gross margin to play with. If that margin doesn't cover your prep and desired net profit, walk away—regardless of what Glass's says the car "should" be worth.

## Summary

The CAP vs. Glass's debate is largely obsolete for the modern, tech-enabled dealer. While CAP remains the essential currency for trade-to-trade transactions, neither book can tell you exactly what a consumer in your specific local area will pay. 

The most profitable dealers in 2026 use CAP to protect their downside at auction, and use live, AI-driven local market data to maximize their upside on the forecourt.
